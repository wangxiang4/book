## 8.Rate of Change Formulas
::: wrap  
1. $a_{\textsf{before}} \xrightarrow{\textsf{increased by }p\%} a_{\textsf{after}}=a_{\textsf{before}}(1+p\%)$  
   $a_{\textsf{before}}=\frac{a_{\textsf{after}}}{1+p\%}$
2. $a_{\textsf{before}} \xrightarrow{\textsf{decreased by }p\%} a_{\textsf{after}}=a_{\textsf{before}}(1-p\%)$  
   $a_{\textsf{before}}=\frac{a_{\textsf{after}}}{1-p\%}$
3. $A\textsf{ is }p\%\textsf{ greater than }B \Longleftrightarrow \frac{A-B}{B}=p\% \Longleftrightarrow A=B(1+p\%)$
4. $A\textsf{ is }p\%\textsf{ less than }B \Longleftrightarrow \frac{B-A}{B}=p\% \Longleftrightarrow A=B(1-p\%)$
5. $A\textsf{ is }p\%\textsf{ of }B \Longleftrightarrow A=B\cdot p\%$
- Note: $A\textsf{ is }p\%\textsf{ greater than }B \ne B\textsf{ is }p\%\textsf{ less than }A$
:::

### $\fbox{11}\:$The planned sales for the store this month are $\small 20\textsf{K USD}$. Because of a promotional event, $\small 60\%$ of the plan has been completed in the first half of the month. If the store needs to exceed the plan by $\small 25\%$ by the end of the month, what amount of sales must be completed in the second half of the month $\small \mathpunct{?}$.
::: wrap  
$
\begin{array}{lllll}
\textsf{(A)} \: 12\textsf{K USD} &
\textsf{(B)} \: 13\textsf{K USD} &
\textsf{(C)} \: 14\textsf{K USD} & \\
\textsf{(D)} \: 15\textsf{K USD} &
\textsf{(E)} \: 16\textsf{K USD} & \\
\end{array}
$  
:::
#### Solution
::: wrap  
$
\begin{array}{ll}
\textsf{sales}_{2\textsf{nd half}}=20(1+25\%)-20\cdot60\% \\
20\cdot1.25-20\cdot0.6 \\
25-12=13 \\
\end{array}
$  
:::  
#### Conclusion
- Derived Solution  
  ::: wrap
  > $\boldsymbol{(B)}$  
  > According to the Solution, get $\textsf{sales}_{2\textsf{nd half}}=13$, so choose $B$. 

  :::
- Formulas used  
  ::: wrap
  >$
  \begin{array}{ll}
  a_{\textsf{after}}=a_{\textsf{before}}(1+p\%) & \href{/math/0%20Core%20Courses/6%20Chapter2%20word%20problems%20Module2%20profit%20in%20commerce.html#_8-rate-of-change-formulas}{\textsf{rate of change formula}} \\

### $\fbox{12}\:$(Sufficiency judgment) The company's per capita cost this year is $\small 60\%$ of last year's.
$\textsf{(1)}$ The company's total cost this year __decreased by $\boldsymbol{25\%}$__, and the number of employees __increased by $\boldsymbol{25\%}$__  
$\textsf{(2)}$ The company's total cost this year __decreased by $\boldsymbol{28\%}$__, and the number of employees __increased by $\boldsymbol{20\%}$__  
$
\begin{array}{lllll}
\textsf{(A)} \: \textsf{(1)} &
\textsf{(B)} \: \textsf{(2)} & \\
\textsf{(C)} \: \textsf{(1)}+\textsf{(2)} &  
\textsf{(D)} \: \textsf{(1)},\textsf{(2)} & \\
\textsf{(E)} \: \varnothing & \\
\end{array}
$  
:::
#### Solution
::: wrap  
$
\begin{array}{ll}
\textcolor{#9333EA}{\textsf{verify condition }\textsf{(1)}} \\
\textsf{last year (let }100\%\textsf{)} \\
\boxed{
\begin{array}{ll}
1 & \textsf{total cost} \\
1 & \textsf{number of employees} \\
1 & \textsf{per capita cost} \\
\end{array}
} \\
\textsf{this year} \\
\boxed{
\begin{array}{ll}
1(1-25\%)=0.75 & \textsf{total cost} \\
1(1+25\%)=1.25 & \textsf{number of employees} \\
\frac{\textsf{total cost}}{\textsf{number of employees}}=0.6 & \textsf{per capita cost} \\
\end{array}
} \\
\textcolor{#9333EA}{\textsf{verify condition }\textsf{(2)}} \\
\textsf{last year (let }100\%\textsf{)} \\
\boxed{
\begin{array}{ll}
1 & \textsf{total cost} \\
1 & \textsf{number of employees} \\
1 & \textsf{per capita cost} \\
\end{array}
} \\
\textsf{this year} \\
\boxed{
\begin{array}{ll}
1(1-28\%)=0.72 & \textsf{total cost} \\
1(1+20\%)=1.2 & \textsf{number of employees} \\
\frac{\textsf{total cost}}{\textsf{number of employees}}=0.6 & \textsf{per capita cost} \\
\end{array}
} \\
\end{array}
$  
:::
#### Conclusion
- Derived Solution  
  ::: wrap
  > $\boldsymbol{(D)}$  
  > According to the Solution, get $\textsf{(1)}\,✅\;,\textsf{(2)}\,✅\;$, so choose $D$.

  :::
- Formulas used  
  ::: wrap
  >$
  \begin{array}{ll}
  \frac{B-A}{B}=p\% \Longleftrightarrow A=B(1-p\%) & \href{/math/0%20Core%20Courses/6%20Chapter2%20word%20problems%20Module2%20profit%20in%20commerce.html#_8-rate-of-change-formulas}{\textsf{rate of change formula}} \\
  \frac{A-B}{B}=p\% \Longleftrightarrow A=B(1+p\%) & \href{/math/0%20Core%20Courses/6%20Chapter2%20word%20problems%20Module2%20profit%20in%20commerce.html#_8-rate-of-change-formulas}{\textsf{rate of change formula}} \\

## 11.Focus 10
__The continuous rate of change__  
- Formula for continuous rate of change:
  if __the original value is $\boldsymbol{a}$__ and __the rate of change is $\boldsymbol{p}$__,
  then __after $\boldsymbol{k}$ consecutive changes__,
  the value becomes $\boldsymbol{a(1+p)^k}$.
- Note: when __the change occurs continuously by month__,
  it only occurs at __the end of each month__,
  so it should be __adjusted to $\boldsymbol{k-1}$__.
  - __Month $\boldsymbol{k}$__: $a(1+p)^{k-1}$
  - __Whole year__: $a+a(1+p)+\cdots+a(1+p)^{11}$  
    ::: wrap  
    $
    \begin{array}{ll}
    S_n=\frac{a_{1}(1-q^n)}{1-q} & \href{/math/0%20Core%20Courses/21%20Chapter5%20sequences%20Module3%20geometric%20sequences.html#_3-sum-of-the-first-terms}{\textsf{geometric series sum formula}} \\
    \frac{a[1-(1+p)^{12}]}{1-(1+p)}=\frac{a[1-(1+p)^{12}]}{1-1-p} \\
    \frac{a[(1+p)^{12}-1]}{p} \\
    \end{array}
    $  
    :::
